osu!

By: Hinature - April 5, 2026

If you have spent any amount of time in the rhythm game community, you have almost certainly heard of osu!. It is one of those games that keeps coming up in every conversation about the genre, and after spending a good amount of time with it myself, I completely understand why. osu! is not just a rhythm game. It is something that can genuinely take over a large portion of your free time if you are not careful.

The main game mode, which most people refer to as osu! standard, has you clicking circles, following sliders, and spinning spinners in time with the music using your mouse or a drawing tablet. The first time I tried it, I thought it looked straightforward enough. A few sessions later, I was humbled. The way the notes are placed across the screen forces you to develop both your aim and your rhythm at the same time, and the difficulty ramps up in a way that keeps pushing you to get better.

One of the things that makes osu! stand out from other rhythm games is the sheer amount of content available. Because the game lets players create and upload their own beatmaps, the library is essentially endless. I spent a lot of time just browsing through beatmaps of songs I already liked, and finding well-made charts for them felt genuinely exciting. There is something special about playing a game to music you actually care about rather than a tracklist you had no say in.


Beyond the standard mode, osu! also has three other modes: osu!taiko, osu!catch, and osu!mania. Each one plays completely differently and has its own community. I personally spent most of my time in the standard mode, but I did try osu!mania for a while and enjoyed it quite a bit. Having that variety within one game is a big plus, especially if you ever feel like switching things up.

I will say though, osu! can feel overwhelming when you are just starting out. The ranking system, the performance points, the modding community — there is a lot going on and it takes some time to understand how everything works. Early on I struggled quite a bit and there were moments where I felt like I was not improving at all. But the moment things started to click and I could feel my aim and timing actually getting sharper, I got hooked in a way that few games manage to do.

Overall, osu! is one of the best rhythm games out there, and the fact that it is completely free makes it even harder to pass up. It has a high skill ceiling, a massive content library, and a competitive community that keeps things interesting. If you have not tried it yet and you enjoy rhythm games at all, I think osu! is worth jumping into.
